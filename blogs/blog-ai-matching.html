We just launched AI Skill Code Matching. Here's what changed.

The biggest update to i.Exchange.ai since launch. Our new matching engine moves beyond keyword overlap to map skills to standardized industry codes.

Today we're shipping the most significant update to i.Exchange.ai since we launched: a completely new AI matching engine built on skill codes rather than keyword overlap. This changes how every match score is calculated — for both job seekers and employers.

Here's what was broken before, what we built, and what it means for you.

The problem with keyword matching

Every major job platform — including most ATS systems used by enterprise HR — works the same way under the hood. They look for the same words in your resume that appear in the job description.

This creates a well-known problem: a candidate who lists "data analysis" doesn't match a role that says "analytics". A developer with "Node.js" experience gets filtered out of a role asking for "backend JavaScript". The skills are identical. The words are different.

In internal testing, we found that keyword-based matching missed an average of 34% of qualified candidates — people who could genuinely do the job but phrased their skills differently.

What we built instead

Our new engine maps both sides — the candidate's experience and the job requirements — to a standardized library of skill codes. These codes are industry-standard identifiers for capabilities, independent of the words used to describe them.

So "data analysis", "analytics", "quantitative analysis", and "data interpretation" all map to the same skill code. The match happens at the code level, not the word level.

How it works in practice

When you upload your resume or describe your experience, our AI does three things:

  • Extracts every skill mentioned, implied, or demonstrated through your experience
  • Maps each skill to one or more standardized skill codes
  • Scores your depth in each skill based on years of use, recency, and context

When an employer posts a role, the same process runs on the job description. The match score you see is the overlap between your skill code profile and the role's skill code requirements — weighted by how important each skill is to the role.

What this means for job seekers

Your match scores will look different from today. In most cases they'll be higher — because the engine now recognizes skills you have that were previously missed due to phrasing. In some cases they'll be lower, because we're now more precise about the depth required for certain roles.

Either way, the score is more accurate. And a more accurate score means you spend your time applying to roles you can actually get — not filtering through irrelevant matches.

What this means for employers

Your shortlists are now ranked on actual skill fit, not resume keyword density. Candidates who wrote their resume in plain language — or who have skills your JD described differently — will now appear in your shortlist when they should.

In our beta test with 40 employers, the new engine surfaced an average of 2.3 additional qualified candidates per role who would have been filtered out by the old keyword system.

What's coming next

This update is the foundation for several features we're building on top of it:

  • Skill trajectory scoring — weighting skills that are growing in demand more heavily
  • Role evolution alerts — notifying you when a role's skill requirements shift in a direction that improves your match
  • Employer skill gap reports — showing hiring teams which skills are hardest to find in their target market

These will roll out over the next two quarters. If you want to be notified when they launch, make sure your profile is active on i.Exchange.ai.

Questions about the new engine or how your score changed? Reach out to us directly through the platform. We read everything.
